Slide 3 – Why Contact Is Complicated

    Contact is not simply two solid bodies touching; it is a tightly coupled problem with several layers of difficulty.

    • Multi-physics. Thermal, electrical, and mechanical fields interact at the interface and must be solved simultaneously.

    • Multi-scale. Surface roughness, trapped third-body particles, and coating layers introduce features from micrometres to millimetres.

    • Tribology. Wear, adhesion, and fretting continually change the interface geometry and material state.

    • Mathematics. The governing conditions are non-linear, non-smooth inequalities, which challenge standard Newton schemes.


Slide 4 – Need for Robust, Fast, Accurate Frameworks

    The complexity just outlined forces us to build numerical frameworks that are simultaneously
    • robust – they converge for rough, highly-coupled interfaces,
    • accurate – they enforce non-penetration and capture near-surface fields,
    • fast – they solve industrial-scale models within practical memory and time.

    Unfortunately, no single method satisfies all three goals in every situation. Each classical approach—penalty, Lagrange multiplier, augmented Lagrangian, FFT half-space—solves part of the puzzle and leaves another part weak.

    To make the trade-offs explicit, the next section reviews these standard strategies and pinpoints their main limitations.


Slide 5 – Signorini–Moreau Contact Conditions

    Mathematically, unilateral friction-free contact is governed by the Signorini–Moreau inequalities.

        Let g(x)g(x) be the normal gap, i.e. the signed distance between the two surfaces.
        g(x)  ≥  0(no penetration)
        g(x)≥0(no penetration)

        Let σn(x)σn​(x) be the normal contact pressure (compression taken as negative).
        σn(x)  ≤  0(no adhesion)
        σn​(x)≤0(no adhesion)

        The two fields are complementary:
        g(x) σn(x)  =  0
        g(x)σn​(x)=0

        If the gap is positive, the pressure is zero; if the pressure is compressive, the gap is zero.

Slide 6 – Classical Numerical Contact Methods

    To enforce the Signorini–Moreau conditions in practice, several classical FEM‐based strategies are available.
    The most common are penalty, Lagrange multiplier, augmented Lagrangian, and FFT half-space formulations.
    Each method targets a different compromise between accuracy, robustness, and computational cost.
    We begin with the simplest to implement—the penalty method.

Slide 7 – Penalty Method

    Idea.
    Replace the non-penetration constraint by a fictitious spring that opposes overlap.

    Constitutive law.
    For a local gap gg:
    σn  =  {−ε g(g<0)penetration0(g≥0)
    σn​={−εg0​(g<0)penetration(g≥0)​

    where ε>0ε>0 is the penalty stiffness.

    Weak form.
    The spring adds the quadratic energy
    Πpen(u)=12 ε∫Γc⟨−g(u)⟩+2 dA,
    Πpen​(u)=21​ε∫Γc​​⟨−g(u)⟩+2​dA,

    which is appended to the elastic potential before taking first variations.

    FEM discretisation.
    No extra unknowns are introduced; the modified stiffness matrix differs from the pure elastic one only by additional diagonal terms on contact nodes.

    Drawback.
    Large εε values reduce penetration but severely ill-condition the global matrix; small values improve conditioning but violate the contact constraint.

Slide 8 – Lagrange Multiplier Method

    Idea.
    Introduce a surface traction field λ as an additional unknown; enforce the gap constraint exactly through a saddle-point functional.

    Functional.
    L(u,λ)=Πelastic(u)+∫Γcλ g(u) dA.
    L(u,λ)=Πelastic​(u)+∫Γc​​λg(u)dA.

    Variation with respect to uu gives equilibrium including λnλn; variation with respect to λλ reproduces the Signorini–Moreau inequalities.

    FEM discretisation.
    Displacements uh=∑Niuiuh​=∑Ni​ui​ are paired with multiplier basis functions MjλjMj​λj​.
    The resulting linear system is
    (K−C ⁣⊤−C0)(uλ)=(fg),
    (K−C​−C⊤0​)(uλ​)=(fg​),

    where KK is positive-definite, CC couples normal displacements to the interface.

    Pros. Exact non-penetration; no penalty parameter.
    Cons. Extra unknowns on ΓcΓc​; matrix is indefinite; inf–sup stability must be respected; iterative or symmetric-indefinite direct solvers are required.

Slide 9 – Augmented Lagrangian Method

    Idea.
    Combine the multiplier with a mild penalty to improve conditioning while still converging to the exact constraint.

    Augmented functional.
    Laug(u,λ)=Πelastic(u)+∫Γc ⁣[ 12εN⟨λ+εNg(u)⟩2−12εNλ2]dA.
    Laug​(u,λ)=Πelastic​(u)+∫Γc​​[2εN​1​⟨λ+εN​g(u)⟩2−2εN​1​λ2]dA.

    For a fixed λλ the problem in uu is positive-definite; λλ is updated by
    λk+1=max⁡(0,  λk+εNg(uk+1))
    λk+1=max(0,λk+εN​g(uk+1))

    (Uzawa or Newton-like loop).

    Numerical properties.
    • Moderate εNεN​ improves matrix conditioning compared with a pure penalty.
    • Exact gap is recovered as the outer loop converges.

    Pros. No inf–sup restriction on interpolation; better conditioning than pure multipliers; penetration eliminated at convergence.
    Cons. Still carries interface unknowns; requires choosing εNεN​; outer iterations add CPU time.
